High-Risk Payment Processing: How to Get Approved Without Getting Shut Down

By Cory Middleton, Head of Growth at Kashu · Updated June 25, 2026

If mainstream processors like Stripe, PayPal, or Square have already turned you down—or threatened to close your account—you’re not alone.

This guide explains what high-risk payment processing really costs, which verticals need it most, the hidden approval rules, and the operational steps that keep your account safe from holds, rolling reserves, and sudden terminations.

What we're working from: Kashu has handled applications from 675 high-risk merchants across 16 verticals — peptides, supplements, e-commerce, CBD-adjacent, credit repair, and more. The patterns below come from that book, not generic advice.

What Is High-Risk Payment Processing?

High-risk payment processing is a specialized merchant account service designed for businesses that process card payments but present elevated risk to acquiring banks and card networks. These risks include higher-than-average chargebacks, regulatory scrutiny, or industry reputation concerns.

Unlike standard accounts, high-risk processors structure fees, reserves, and monitoring to offset the elevated exposure. They are not “guaranteed” approvals; instead, they price the risk into effective discount rates of roughly 3.5%–4.95% plus a small per-transaction fee (often $0.20–$0.30).

Common verticals include dietary supplements, CBD and hemp-derived products, peptide sellers, subscription boxes, and e-commerce stores with high-ticket or recurring billing.

  • Dietary supplement merchants often face scrutiny under FDA labeling rules and high chargeback rates from dissatisfied customers.
  • CBD merchants are flagged due to the complex patchwork of state and federal laws, even where hemp is legal under the 2018 Farm Bill.
  • Subscription models trigger elevated risk because of free-to-paid conversion disputes and continuous billing complaints.

Why Do Stripe, PayPal, and Square Reject These Businesses?

Mainstream processors operate under strict card-network rules and investor mandates that cap chargeback ratios and fraud losses. When your industry profile, chargeback history, or product type exceeds their risk appetite, they decline or terminate your account to protect their network performance metrics.

For example, Visa’s Acquirer Monitoring Program (VAMP) flags acquirers when merchant portfolios exceed a 0.9% dispute ratio. Aggregators like Stripe can hold funds for up to 180 days and close accounts without notice if they detect elevated risk.

Regulatory uncertainty—such as the FDA’s evolving stance on CBD labeling or FTC scrutiny of supplement claims—also increases perceived risk and reduces the willingness of standard processors to onboard these merchants.

Who Actually Needs High-Risk Payment Processing?

You likely need high-risk processing if you operate in a heavily scrutinized vertical, process a high volume of transactions, or have a history of chargebacks or refunds. Merchants in supplements, CBD, peptides, adult content, vape, or high-ticket e-commerce often fall into this category.

Subscription businesses with free trials or negative-option billing also require high-risk accounts because recurring billing increases dispute rates. Similarly, merchants processing international sales or high-ticket items can trigger stricter underwriting.

If you’ve been rejected by two or more mainstream processors in the past 12 months, or if your average ticket exceeds $500, you should prepare for high-risk underwriting.

The Real Approval Criteria Behind High-Risk Underwriting

High-risk underwriting evaluates business fundamentals, compliance posture, financials, and operational controls—not just your industry. Processors look for documented Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for chargeback management, clear refund and return policies, and evidence that you vet customers and monitor transactions for fraud.

They also assess your processing history: time in business, average monthly volume (AMV), chargeback ratio, and whether you’ve had prior account terminations or holds. Strong credit scores and clean banking relationships improve your odds.

For regulated products like CBD, processors require third-party compliance certifications (e.g., US Hemp Authority seal), lab reports (COAs), and proof of state licenses where applicable.

  • Underwriters typically ask for 3–6 months of bank statements, a voided check or business license, and a signed merchant application with full disclosure of all business activities.

How Much Does High-Risk Processing Really Cost?

High-risk accounts are priced with an effective discount rate (blended rate) of roughly 3.5%–4.95% plus a per-transaction fee (often $0.20–$0.30). This is higher than standard interchange-plus pricing due to the elevated risk and reserve requirements.

Most processors also impose a rolling reserve—typically 5%–10% of your monthly processing volume—held for 90–180 days to cover potential chargebacks. Some use a fixed reserve or a percentage-based hold that releases over time as your dispute ratio improves.

Multiple Merchant IDs (MIDs) and load-balancing across processors can reduce reserve percentages and improve stability, but they also increase operational complexity and may carry additional setup fees.

Common Reasons Applications Get Declined—and How to Fix Them

Incomplete or inconsistent applications are a top reason for decline. Missing documents, mismatched business names, or undisclosed related entities raise red flags and trigger manual reviews that often end in rejection.

High chargeback ratios—especially above 2%—or a prior account termination signal systemic risk and disqualify most applicants under mainstream or high-risk underwriting guidelines.

Weak compliance controls, such as lacking refund policies, missing age verification for restricted products, or failing to display required disclaimers, can result in automatic declines under Visa’s Global Brand Rules or Mastercard’s Risk Standards.

To improve your application, prepare a clean business profile with consistent documentation, a detailed chargeback mitigation plan, and third-party compliance evidence where applicable. Consider working with a high-risk specialist who understands how to present your business to underwriters.

Keeping Your High-Risk Account Healthy: Chargebacks, VAMP, and Monitoring

Once approved, your biggest risk is chargebacks. Visa’s VAMP monitors acquirers when merchant dispute ratios exceed 0.9%. If your ratio climbs above that threshold, your processor may impose fines, increase reserves, or even terminate your account.

Proactive defense includes clear customer communications, easy cancellation policies for subscriptions, and a documented dispute response workflow. Use tools like Ethoca alerts and Verifi’s Order Insight to resolve disputes before they become chargebacks.

Maintain a dispute ratio below 1.5% and aim for rapid response times—under 48 hours—to avoid escalation to pre-arbitration or arbitration, which can trigger additional fees and longer fund holds.

Regularly audit your chargeback sources: identify friendly fraud, merchant errors, and criminal fraud. Correct operational gaps like unclear billing descriptors, delayed refunds, or poor customer service that drive disputes.

Vertical-Specific Tips to Improve Approval Odds

For CBD merchants: obtain a US Hemp Authority seal, maintain current COAs from ISO/IEC 17025 labs, and display a compliant label with THC content under 0.3%. Document your supply chain and third-party lab testing records to prove regulatory adherence.

For supplement merchants: avoid implied health claims in marketing and include FDA disclaimers. Maintain a clean refund policy and age verification for any age-restricted products. Monitor customer complaints and respond quickly to prevent escalation.

For subscription businesses: implement clear free-to-paid conversion messaging, easy cancellation flows, and proactive billing reminders. Use dunning management to reduce involuntary churn and disputes from failed payments.

For peptides: ensure your products are not classified as unapproved drugs and maintain documentation showing they are research chemicals or veterinary products, not human drugs.

Alternatives and Exit Strategies

If high-risk processing proves too costly or restrictive, consider splitting your payment flow across multiple processors or using a payment facilitator model with stricter controls. Some merchants use separate accounts for domestic vs. international sales to reduce reserve requirements.

Another option is to rebrand or spin off a lower-risk segment of your business (e.g., selling accessories or non-CBD merchandise) under a separate entity with a standard merchant account. This can reduce overall exposure but requires legal separation and careful compliance.

Over time, as your dispute ratio stabilizes below 1% and your compliance posture strengthens, you may qualify for a mid-tier processor with lower fees. Document your improvements and negotiate with your current provider for better terms.

Frequently asked questions

What is a rolling reserve and how long does it last?

A rolling reserve is a portion of your monthly sales held back by your processor to cover potential chargebacks. It typically ranges from 5% to 10% and is held for 90 to 180 days. The reserve releases gradually as your dispute history improves and your chargeback ratio remains low.

Can I use Stripe or PayPal if I switch to a high-risk processor?

Some merchants maintain a secondary account with a standard processor for low-risk product lines or small transactions. However, using Stripe or PayPal for high-risk sales violates their terms of service and risks account closure or fund seizure. Always keep accounts separate and compliant.

How do I lower my effective discount rate over time?

Focus on reducing chargebacks and improving your dispute ratio. Processors may reduce your reserve or lower your rate after 6–12 months of stable performance. You can also negotiate pricing as your volume grows or consolidate processing under a single high-risk provider with tiered pricing.

Are there processors that specialize in peptides or research chemicals?

Yes. Several high-risk specialists focus on research chemical and peptide merchants, provided the products are marketed and sold as non-consumable research compounds. Underwriters will scrutinize labeling, marketing claims, and any indication of human consumption.

What happens if my chargeback ratio exceeds 0.9%?

If your dispute ratio exceeds 0.9%, Visa’s VAMP triggers monitoring. Your processor may increase your rolling reserve, impose fines, or place you into remediation. Persistent non-compliance can lead to account termination or placement on Visa’s MATCH list, which restricts your ability to open new merchant accounts.
