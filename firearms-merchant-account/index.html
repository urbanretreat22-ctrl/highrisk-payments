How to Get a Firearms & Ammunition Merchant Account Without Getting Shut Down

By Cory Middleton, Head of Growth at Kashu · Updated June 25, 2026

If Stripe, PayPal or Square have already turned down your gun-store application—or worse, frozen your funds after months of sales—you’re not alone.

This guide shows you exactly how a dedicated firearms & ammunition merchant account works, why mainstream processors blacklist the category, what underwriters really look for, and the realistic pricing, reserve and integration steps that keep your doors open without surprise terminations.

What we're working from: Kashu has handled applications from 675 high-risk merchants across 16 verticals — peptides, supplements, e-commerce, CBD-adjacent, credit repair, and more. The patterns below come from that book, not generic advice.

What a Firearms & Ammunition Merchant Account Actually Is

A firearms & ammunition merchant account is a specialized credit-card processing relationship that lets federally licensed FFL dealers accept Visa, Mastercard, Discover and Amex for gun and ammo sales. Because the card networks label the vertical as “high risk,” these accounts carry higher effective discount rates, rolling reserves, and stricter monitoring rules than a standard retail account. Merchants in this space must already hold an active Federal Firearms License (FFL) and comply with state and federal laws before an acquirer will board the account.

Many processors misclassify FFL merchants under the generic MCC 5999 (tobacco, cigars, e-cigarettes) instead of the correct firearms/ammunition descriptors. That misclassification triggers automatic fraud filters and can push chargeback ratios above network thresholds even when your actual dispute volume is low. A proper firearms merchant account will use the correct business description and merchant category codes so your transactions route through the right monitoring programs rather than generic high-risk tiers.

  • Correct business descriptors include “Retail Firearms and Ammunition Dealer (FFL)”, “Ammunition Sales (FFL)”, or “Gunsmith & Firearms Accessories.”

Why Mainstream Processors Reject—or Shut Down—Firearms & Ammo Sellers

Card networks (Visa, Mastercard, Discover) classify firearms and ammunition retailers as high risk because the category carries elevated chargeback and fraud risk tied to age-verification failures, regulatory compliance mistakes, and the resale nature of some transactions. Aggregators like Stripe, PayPal and Square have publicly stated policies banning the sale, advertising, or facilitation of firearms, ammunition, or related accessories, regardless of FFL status. Even if you have a valid FFL, accepting cards through these platforms can result in sudden account closures, rolling reserves held for up to 180 days, and clawbacks of prior settlements.

Processor bans are not based on a single event; they stem from the card networks’ merchant category risk ratings and the acquirers’ own risk appetite. After high-profile shootings and regulatory scrutiny, acquirers tightened underwriting for FFL merchants, raising rolling-reserve requirements from 5% to 10% and increasing effective discount rates by 0.75%–1.25%. Some legacy high-risk providers have also exited the space, creating a smaller pool of boarding options and longer approval timelines.

Who Really Needs a Firearms & Ammunition Merchant Account

Any FFL holder who accepts credit or debit cards for firearms, ammunition, reloading components, or gun safes needs a dedicated firearms merchant account. Brick-and-mortar gun stores, online FFL dealers, ammunition wholesalers, gunsmiths, and even ranges that sell ammo by the case rely on these accounts because mainstream processors refuse the category. If you attempt to process under a non-firearms MCC or disguise your business description, you risk forced downgrades, excessive chargebacks, and sudden account terminations that freeze all payouts indefinitely.

FFL manufacturers and importers also need specialized accounts when they sell directly to consumers or through dealer networks. Because these entities often deal in high-ticket items and drop-ship logistics, underwriters require additional documentation of compliance SOPs, inventory controls, and third-party age-verification integrations before approval.

The Real Approval Criteria Underwriters Use Today

Underwriters evaluate FFL merchants on five pillars: FFL validity and compliance history, product mix, transaction velocity and average ticket, chargeback and fraud history, and merchant controls. Your FFL must be current, unencumbered, and issued by the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF); any prior compliance violations can trigger additional scrutiny or denial. Product mix matters: accessories like holsters or cleaning kits are easier to board than handguns or semi-auto rifles, which are scrutinized more closely.

Transaction velocity and average ticket size affect reserve levels and pricing. Merchants processing more than $50,000/month with average tickets above $1,000 typically face 10% rolling reserves and higher effective discount rates, while smaller stores may qualify for 5% reserves and slightly lower rates. Underwriters also pull public records for civil judgments, injunctions, or prior merchant-account terminations; any red flags in your BSA/AML history or OFAC screening can delay or block approval.

Documentation requirements include a completed high-risk application, voided check or bank letter, processing statements from the last three months (if you have an existing account), FFL license front and back, ATF Form 4473 sample, and a written Age Verification SOP that integrates with your e-commerce platform or POS. If you sell online, you must also provide SSL certificate details, PCI DSS SAQ or ROC, and a clear refund and return policy that complies with federal and state laws.

Pricing, Rolling Reserves, and Multi-MID Strategies

Expect an effective discount rate of roughly 3.95%–4.95% plus a per-transaction fee of $0.20–$0.35 when you board with a firearms-specialized acquirer. These rates include the interchange cost, network assessments, the acquirer’s margin, and the risk premium baked into the program. Rolling reserves typically run 5%–10% of daily settlement volume and release after 180 days of clean performance; some newer providers offer graduated reserve release tied to chargeback ratio milestones.

Because aggregators and some high-risk providers cap monthly volume on a single MID, successful FFL merchants often load-balance across two to three MIDs using separate gateways or processors. This strategy reduces the impact of a single account freeze and can improve approval odds when you apply under different business entities or DBAs. Each MID will carry its own rolling reserve and effective discount rate, so you should model blended cost before committing to multiple accounts.

Why Applications Get Declined—and How to Fix Them

The most common denial reasons for firearms & ammo merchants are invalid or expired FFL, missing age-verification controls, high chargeback ratios on prior accounts, or product categories that violate card-network policies (e.g., armor-piercing ammo, silencers, or short-barrel rifles). Underwriters also decline applications with unclear refund policies, lack of PCI DSS compliance, or prior merchant-account terminations within the last 24 months. If you’re declined, ask for the specific reason in writing; you can then remediate and reapply with corrected documentation.

If your FFL is valid but your chargeback ratio exceeded 0.9% in the last three months, consider pausing high-risk categories like handguns or suppressors, implementing stricter AVS/CVV checks, and adding a clear age-gate on your website before reapplying. Some providers allow a “clean-room” application where you omit certain product lines initially, then expand after 90 days of compliant performance. Be transparent about any past regulatory actions; omitting them usually leads to a second, harsher denial.

Keeping the Account Healthy—Chargebacks, VAMP, and Compliance

Once boarded, the biggest risk is the Visa Acquirer Monitoring Program (VAMP), which flags acquirers whose merchants exceed a 0.9% dispute ratio. If your ratio climbs above 1.0%, Visa can impose fines, mandatory remediation plans, or even terminate your MID, freezing funds for up to 180 days. To stay below the threshold, integrate real-time age verification at checkout, store ATF Forms 4473 digitally with tamper-evident logs, and maintain a clear paper trail for every firearm transfer.

Implement a proactive chargeback defense workflow: use 3D Secure (3DS) authentication for card-not-present transactions, set clear product descriptions that match cardholder statements, and respond to retrieval requests within five calendar days. Train staff to verify customer IDs against state POC (point-of-contact) databases and maintain a written log of age-verification steps. Some firearms processors also offer chargeback alerts and representment services—factor that cost into your effective rate when comparing providers.

  • Card-network rules require you to retain ATF Forms 4473 for 20 years; digital storage must be encrypted, immutable, and searchable.

Step-by-Step Application Checklist

Gather your FFL license (front and back), EIN or SSN, voided check or bank letter, and three months of bank/processor statements. Prepare your Age Verification SOP, refund and return policy, PCI DSS compliance document (SAQ or ROC), and SSL certificate details for e-commerce stores. If you sell online, include your shopping-cart platform and gateway integrations; underwriters prefer Shopify Plus, BigCommerce Enterprise, or WooCommerce with specific fraud modules enabled.

Draft product-category lists that separate low-risk accessories from high-risk firearms and ammo; some providers allow you to start with accessories only and add higher-risk categories later after clean performance. Submit your application through a firearms-specialized high-risk provider; avoid generic high-risk brokers who don’t understand FFL nuances—misclassification leads to immediate monitoring flags.

Expect a manual underwriting review that can take 7–14 business days. If approved, you’ll receive your MID, gateway credentials, and reserve schedule. Schedule a compliance call with your new acquirer to review VAMP thresholds, chargeback response timelines, and reserve release milestones before your first settlement.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use Stripe Atlas or Square for an FFL business if I set up a separate entity?

No. Both Stripe and Square explicitly prohibit firearms, ammunition, and related accessories in their Acceptable Use Policies. Attempting to process under a non-firearms entity or rebranding the business will trigger automated fraud filters and likely result in account closure and fund holds up to 180 days. Maintain a dedicated firearms merchant account to stay compliant.

What does MCC 5999 misclassification mean for my gun store?

MCC 5999 is the generic code for “Durable Nondurable Goods,” which card networks associate with tobacco, cigars, and vaping. When a firearms merchant is misclassified under MCC 5999 instead of the correct business descriptor, transactions route through generic high-risk monitoring instead of the specialized firearms compliance stream. This increases false positives in fraud filters, raises dispute ratios, and can push your account into Visa’s Acquirer Monitoring Program even when your actual dispute volume is low.

How long does a rolling reserve last for an FFL merchant?

Rolling reserves typically run 5%–10% of daily settlement volume and release after 180 days of clean performance. Some newer providers offer graduated release tied to chargeback ratio milestones—e.g., 50% release at 0.6% ratio for 90 consecutive days, full release at 0.3% for six months. Reserve schedules are negotiated during underwriting and are non-negotiable for firearms merchants.

Do I need a separate merchant account for online vs. in-store sales?

Not necessarily, but many FFL merchants use separate MIDs for online and retail to isolate risk and simplify PCI scoping. Each MID will carry its own rolling reserve and effective discount rate. If you process predominantly online, ensure your gateway supports 3D Secure and AVS/CVV checks; if in-store, use a PCI-validated POS with end-to-end encryption.

What happens if my chargeback ratio exceeds 0.9% under VAMP?

If your dispute ratio exceeds 0.9%, Visa can impose fines, mandatory remediation plans, or even terminate your MID, freezing funds for up to 180 days. Your acquirer may also place you in a chargeback monitoring program with stricter response deadlines and higher reserves. To mitigate, implement real-time age verification, 3D Secure, and proactive retrieval-response workflows before disputes escalate.
