High-Risk Merchant Accounts: How to Get Approved Without Getting Shut Down

By Cory Middleton, Head of Growth at Kashu · Updated June 25, 2026

If mainstream processors keep rejecting your applications — or worse, approved you and then froze your funds — you don't need another aggregator. You need a high-risk merchant account: an account that's individually underwritten by a bank that knowingly accepts your business type, set up so it not only approves but survives. This guide covers what a high-risk merchant account actually is, who needs one, the real approval criteria, what it costs, and how to keep it from getting terminated — written from what we see boarding these businesses every day.

What we're working from: Kashu has handled applications from 675 high-risk merchants across 16 verticals — peptides, supplements, e-commerce, CBD-adjacent, credit repair, and more. The patterns below come from that book, not generic advice.

What is a high-risk merchant account?

Stripe, PayPal, and Square are payment aggregators. They onboard millions of merchants under one shared set of acceptable-use rules, which is why approval is instant — nobody underwrote you individually. It's also why you get shut off the moment a review flags your products or a chargeback spike appears, with funds held up to 180 days under their terms.

A high-risk merchant account is the opposite: a dedicated account, individually underwritten, placed with an acquiring bank that accepts your category on purpose. It's boarded under a high-risk MCC, priced for the real risk (a discount rate in the 3.5–4.95% range), and usually carries a rolling reserve. You pay more, and you keep your business running — for most high-risk merchants, that trade isn't close.

Who needs one — the high-risk verticals

If an aggregator has frozen or declined you, you almost certainly fall into a category they won't keep. The most common high-risk verticals we board:

Peptides & research compounds Nutraceuticals & supplements CBD & hemp Credit repair Subscription / continuity High-chargeback e-commerce

The common thread isn't that these businesses are illegitimate — it's that they carry elevated chargeback exposure or sit in a category the card networks watch closely, so generalist processors simply won't keep them. That refusal is exactly why a specialist account exists.

Why mainstream processors shut high-risk businesses down

It's a policy decision, not a billing error. Aggregators prohibit high-risk categories in their acceptable-use terms, and the card networks have tightened high-risk and supplement enforcement through 2025–2026. When a review or a chargeback spike triggers it, the aggregator freezes the account and the original termination follows your business details — so re-applying to the same processor almost never works. The fix is a different kind of account, not another attempt at the same door.

The real approval criteria (what underwriters actually check)

1. A clean, honest business profile

High-risk underwriting is individual. Underwriters want a registered entity, a live website that matches your stated model, and — critically — full disclosure of any prior processor terminations up front. They find them anyway; late disclosure is what kills trust and approvals.

2. Documentation that fits your vertical

Depending on category, that means current product documentation (for supplements/peptides, third-party certificates of analysis and clear research-use labeling), licensing where applicable, and clear, non-deceptive product claims. Overstating what a product does is the fastest route to a decline.

3. Processing history, if you have it

Three to six months of prior statements show real volume and your true chargeback rate. No history isn't disqualifying, but a clean history materially speeds approval.

4. Reserves and pricing you can plan around

Expect a rolling reserve, commonly 5–10% held and released on a rolling schedule, plus a discount rate in the 3.5–4.95% range. Reserves are normal and temporary — treat them as working-capital timing, not a loss.

How high-risk payment processing pricing and stability actually work

High-risk payment processing is priced differently from a flat-rate aggregator, and understanding the structure helps you plan cash flow instead of being surprised by it. Three pieces matter:

The discount rate

Your effective rate typically lands in the 3.5–4.95% range, versus the ~2.9% + 30¢ an aggregator advertises. The gap isn't a markup for its own sake — it reflects the genuine chargeback and regulatory cost of the category, and it buys you an account that won't be frozen the first time a review runs. Rates move with your vertical, your processing history, and your chargeback ratio, so a clean history is worth real basis points.

The rolling reserve

Most high-risk accounts carry a rolling reserve — commonly 5–10% of volume held and released on a rolling schedule (for example, held for 180 days, then released as new volume rotates in). It feels like a cost, but it's a timing mechanism: the money is yours, it's just released on a delay to cover potential disputes. Build it into your working-capital planning and it stops being a surprise.

Multiple MIDs and load balancing

For higher-volume merchants, the difference between a stable processor and a fragile one is often redundancy. Spreading volume across multiple merchant IDs (MIDs) means a single acquirer issue or a chargeback spike on one MID doesn't take down your entire ability to process. This is the structural reason a purpose-built high-risk setup survives where an aggregator doesn't — it's designed for the volatility of the category instead of pretending it away.

Put together, high-risk payment processing costs more per transaction but is built to keep running. For a business that's already been deplatformed once, the price of stability is the easiest line item to justify.

Keeping the account healthy so it doesn't happen again

Getting approved is half the job. High-risk accounts get terminated after approval too — usually for the same reason aggregators cut you off: chargebacks. The card networks watch this directly. Visa's acquirer monitoring program flags merchants once disputes climb toward roughly 0.9% of transactions, and sustained breaches put your account and your acquiring relationship at risk.

The merchants who keep stable processing tend to do the same handful of things:

  • A clear billing descriptor so customers recognize the charge (a big share of disputes are simply "I don't recognize this").
  • Fast, visible support — a reachable refund path means customers ask you before they ask their bank.
  • Honest product pages — overselling is what generates the disputes that get you terminated.
  • Watching the ratio, not just the count — dispute rate is a percentage of volume; track it like a vital sign.

Stable high-risk processing isn't luck. It's the right account boarded to the right model, plus disciplined dispute hygiene so you never drift toward the thresholds that trigger a shutdown.

Frequently asked questions

What is a high-risk merchant account?

An individually-underwritten payment account placed with a bank that knowingly accepts high-risk categories — boarded under a high-risk MCC, usually with a 5–10% rolling reserve and a 3.5–4.95% rate.

Why did Stripe/PayPal/Square shut me down?

Their acceptable-use policies prohibit high-risk categories. They freeze on review or after chargebacks and can hold funds up to 180 days. Re-applying to the same processor rarely works.

What businesses need one?

Nutraceuticals, supplements, peptides, CBD, credit repair, subscription billing, firearms, adult, gaming, travel, and high-chargeback e-commerce, among others.

How do I get approved?

Through individual underwriting: a registered entity, a site that matches your model, the right documentation, and up-front disclosure of any prior terminations.
