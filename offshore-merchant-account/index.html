Get an offshore merchant account that actually stays open—legitimate options, trade-offs & approval tips

By Cory Middleton, Head of Growth at Kashu · Updated June 25, 2026

If your business runs on crypto, CBD, adult, or other high-risk activities, mainstream processors like Stripe, PayPal, and Square have already told you “no,” often without explanation.

This guide explains what an offshore merchant account really is, why traditional processors reject these businesses, which jurisdictions give the best odds, the real approval criteria, pricing mechanics, and how to keep the account healthy once you’re approved.

What we're working from: Kashu has handled applications from 675 high-risk merchants across 16 verticals — peptides, supplements, e-commerce, CBD-adjacent, credit repair, and more. The patterns below come from that book, not generic advice.

What an offshore merchant account is—and isn’t

An offshore merchant account is a payment-processing facility established outside the merchant’s home country, allowing businesses in restricted or high-risk verticals to accept card payments without relying on domestic acquirers.

It is not a loophole to bypass consumer-protection rules; it is a legitimate banking arrangement that operates under different regulatory frameworks, often in jurisdictions that specialize in serving high-risk or cross-border merchants.

The account is opened through an acquiring bank or a licensed payment facilitator whose domicile is outside the merchant’s home jurisdiction, with settlement routed to an offshore or domestic account depending on the provider’s structure.

Offshore accounts are commonly used by businesses in adult entertainment, online gaming, CBD, nutraceuticals, crypto exchanges, and certain international e-commerce models that domestic acquirers deem too risky or non-compliant under local card-network rules.

Why mainstream processors reject or shut down these businesses

Domestic processors avoid high-chargeback verticals because card networks impose penalties (fines, monitoring programs) when merchant dispute ratios exceed 0.9%, and they cannot risk absorbing the liability for entire portfolios.

Aggregators like Stripe and Square operate under strict card-network rules that cap their aggregate chargeback rates; when a single vertical’s chargeback profile threatens the whole portfolio, they shut down entire categories rather than risk VAMP fines under the Visa Acquirer Monitoring Program.

Offshore acquirers, by contrast, operate under jurisdictions with lower enforcement intensity and can price for higher risk, making them willing to onboard merchants whose domestic counterparts reject outright.

Regulatory arbitrage does not eliminate card-network rules entirely, but it shifts some of the compliance burden to the offshore acquirer, who must itself manage its own monitoring thresholds and reserve requirements.

Who really needs an offshore merchant account—and who doesn’t

Businesses with high domestic chargeback exposure—above 1% of transactions or with frequent “friendly fraud” disputes—are prime candidates because domestic acquirers terminate accounts preemptively to protect their own compliance scores.

International merchants serving multiple currencies and regulatory regimes often find domestic acquirers unwilling to support local payment methods or multi-currency settlement without excessive reserves or rolling holds.

High-risk verticals such as online gaming, CBD, vape, adult, and certain crypto-related services are systematically excluded by domestic processors in many jurisdictions despite being legal under local law.

Merchants with complex ownership structures or beneficial owners in high-risk countries may trigger enhanced due-diligence triggers that domestic acquirers cannot accommodate within their KYC frameworks.

Conversely, a U.S.-based CBD brand selling only domestically with low dispute rates may qualify for a domestic high-risk program; offshore is only necessary if domestic options fail or if the business needs multi-currency settlement.

Jurisdictions that work: real options and trade-offs

Belize and the British Virgin Islands are popular for their English common-law systems and professional banking relationships, but Belize’s International Financial Services Commission requires licensed entities and can take 60–90 days for full approval.

Estonia remains the EU’s leading fintech hub for high-risk merchants; it offers an EMI license and access to SEPA, but requires a local compliance officer, a physical office presence, and ongoing AML/CFT reporting under EU directives.

Cyprus, once a favored destination, tightened its high-risk policies after EU AML audits; now it primarily serves licensed EMIs rather than direct merchant onboarding, adding layers to the structure.

Singapore and Labuan (Malaysia) offer strong banking ties and English-speaking compliance teams, but Labuan’s 3% gross-revenue tax and higher setup costs make it less accessible for startups.

Costa Rica and Panama provide regional access with lower setup costs, but their card-network compliance frameworks lag EU/US standards, which can create downstream issues with domestic banks or card issuers.

Gibraltar’s GFSC-regulated EMIs are widely used for crypto and gaming, but Gibraltar’s high fixed compliance costs (€50k–€100k annually) push smaller merchants toward aggregators or offshore facilitators instead.

How to actually qualify: the real approval criteria

The offshore underwriter evaluates the business model, chargeback history, refund rate, average ticket, and the merchant’s ability to absorb rolling reserves—typically 5–10% held for 180 days under Visa rules.

Beneficial ownership must be fully disclosed; complex structures with PEP flags or high-risk countries trigger enhanced due diligence and can result in immediate declines if not mitigated with additional documentation.

Processing volumes and ticket sizes must align with the acquirer’s risk appetite; a merchant processing $20k/month with $100 tickets is treated differently from one doing $2M/month with $5 tickets and higher dispute exposure.

Underwriters require proof of legal business registration, tax filings, bank statements, and sometimes a compliance audit from a recognized provider such as LegitScript or TrustedSource to substantiate product claims and marketing practices.

The website and checkout flow must display clear refund policies, age verification where applicable, and accurate merchant descriptors to reduce chargebacks; hidden or misleading descriptors correlate with higher dispute rates and trigger VAMP scrutiny.

Pricing, reserves, and structuring for sustainability

Offshore acquirers price using an effective discount rate that typically ranges from 3.5% to 4.95% plus a small per-transaction fee (commonly $0.20–$0.35), reflecting the higher risk and compliance overhead.

Rolling reserves are commonly set at 5–10% of monthly processing volume and held for up to 180 days; this reserve is released only after the acquirer’s chargeback window closes without excessive disputes.

Some providers offer multiple merchant IDs (MIDs) or load-balancing across different acquiring banks to reduce concentration risk and improve approval odds, though each MID may carry its own reserve and fee schedule.

Settlement currency and payout rails matter: USD payouts to U.S. banks are easiest; EUR to SEPA accounts is efficient in Europe; crypto settlement introduces additional volatility and compliance checks that can slow funding.

Be wary of “teaser” rates that revert to 6–8% after three months; request a full fee schedule and reserve policy in writing before signing any agreement.

Why applications get declined—and how to fix it

Incomplete KYC—missing beneficial-ownership forms, unverified bank statements, or outdated legal documents—triggers automatic declines under offshore underwriting guidelines aligned with FATF Recommendation 24.

High chargeback ratios—even from past processors—remain the single largest reason for decline; underwriters will demand a remediation plan, chargeback insurance, or a lower processing limit to reconsider.

Product or marketing misalignment—unsubstantiated health claims, lack of age verification, or unclear refund policies—flags the account as deceptive under card-network rules, leading to immediate rejection.

Geographic exposure—beneficial owners or processing traffic from OFAC-sanctioned countries, or high-risk jurisdictions without proper licensing—can trigger automatic declines or extended due diligence cycles.

If declined for KYC gaps, provide notarized identity documents, updated bank statements, and a signed beneficial-ownership disclosure. If declined for chargebacks, submit a chargeback management plan, third-party dispute defense, and a lower volume cap for probationary approval.

Keeping the account healthy: chargebacks, VAMP, and renewals

Monitor dispute ratios weekly; if they approach 0.7%, implement pre-dispute alerts, improve customer service, and tighten refund policies to stay below the 0.9% VAMP threshold that triggers acquirer penalties.

Use a dedicated chargeback management platform with representment tools; many offshore acquirers require merchants to handle their own disputes or pay steep chargeback fees if ratios exceed 1%.

Keep merchant descriptors clear and consistent; mismatched billing names or vague descriptors correlate with higher dispute rates and can trigger VAMP alerts even if the merchant’s dispute ratio is low.

Renewal cycles typically occur annually; underwriters re-evaluate chargeback ratios, product compliance, and beneficial-ownership changes; maintaining clean records and responsive compliance contacts improves renewal odds.

If you breach VAMP thresholds, expect a reserve increase or a rolling hold; consistent breaches can lead to contract termination and a 24–36 month cooling-off period before reapplying with another offshore provider.

Frequently asked questions

What’s the difference between an offshore merchant account and a domestic high-risk account?

A domestic high-risk account is opened by a licensed acquirer in the merchant’s home country under domestic card-network rules, while an offshore merchant account is opened by an entity outside that country, often under different regulatory and compliance regimes.

Can an offshore merchant account process credit cards in the U.S. legally?

Yes, provided the offshore acquirer is licensed and the merchant complies with U.S. card-network rules and OFAC screening; the account simply operates under foreign regulatory oversight rather than domestic.

How long does it take to open an offshore merchant account?

Typical timelines range from 30 days for straightforward cases to 90 days for complex structures or jurisdictions requiring licensed entities; incomplete KYC or high-risk verticals extend the process.

Do offshore accounts have higher fees than domestic high-risk accounts?

Often yes; offshore acquirers price for higher risk and compliance overhead, resulting in effective discount rates of 3.5%–4.95% plus reserves, compared to domestic high-risk programs that may start around 2.9%–3.5%.

Can I avoid rolling reserves with an offshore merchant account?

Rolling reserves are standard in high-risk offshore programs; some providers offer tiered or decreasing reserves after 6–12 months of clean performance, but they are rarely eliminated entirely for high-risk verticals.
